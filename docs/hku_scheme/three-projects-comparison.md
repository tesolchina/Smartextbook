## Part 2 — Simon's Role Across All Three Projects

Simon is not the "subject expert" in any of the three projects. His role is consistent across all of them:

**He is the pedagogical philosophy carrier + technical enabler.**

In each project, there is a discipline expert who owns the course content:
- Krista Du owns career coaching in Chinese workplace contexts
- The journalism teacher owns data journalism analysis
- The GTSU instructor owns European diplomacy and peace studies

Simon contributes two things that none of these experts have:

**1. The technical capability** — He builds the platforms (SmartTextbook, MartinPeaceBot, and whatever the journalism project uses). Without him, these ideas remain ideas. With him, they become deployable tools in real courses.

**2. The pedagogical philosophy** — This is what travels across all three projects and stays consistent regardless of subject. He brings the same set of convictions about how AI should work in education every time.

This is the right way to describe his role on any submission: not "co-teacher of Chinese for Workplace Applications" but "AI-in-education specialist and Innovation Officer enabling cross-disciplinary pedagogical research."

## Part 3 — The Shared Philosophy

All three projects, despite being in completely different disciplines, share the same underlying convictions. These are not Simon's private preferences — they are a coherent educational philosophy that can be named and cited.

### Conviction 1: AI should deepen thinking, not replace it

In every project, the AI is designed to ask rather than tell. The Research Advisor never fabricates diplomatic facts. The Career Coaching system never writes the CV. Bess's data journalism scaffold asks students to identify instruments, not provides the identification for them.

This is the direct opposite of how most students use ChatGPT. The projects are unified by a specific critique: that unguided AI use produces what Ahmedtelba et al. (2025) call "unproductive cognitive offloading" — fluent output that substitutes for genuine skill development.

The antidote in all three projects is **proactive Socratic scaffolding**: AI that responds to student input with a question that advances their thinking rather than content that replaces it.

### Conviction 2: The expert–novice gap is specific, not general

Each project starts from a concrete analysis of what experts do that novices miss:
- **Career coaching:** Industry-aligned CVs require cultural contextualisation that generic AI cannot provide
- **Data journalism:** Bess names AirBeam2, PurpleAir PA-II, DustTrak II 8530; students write "sensors were used"
- **Diplomacy:** Expert position papers distinguish official stated positions from actual strategic interests; student work does not

This specificity is what makes the AI scaffolding possible. You cannot build a Socratic tutor around a vague problem. All three projects succeed in defining the gap precisely enough that an AI can be prompted to guide students across it.

### Conviction 3: Student knowledge is a design resource, not just a target

In all three projects, students bring something to the design table that teachers cannot:
- **Career coaching:** Students know what it feels like to receive generic AI feedback on a CV. Teachers have never been on the receiving end. Students co-design the rubric criteria based on that lived experience.
- **Data journalism:** Bess's exemplary analysis is not just an example — it is the intellectual foundation of the entire scaffolding system. A student's expert thinking shapes how AI guides future students.
- **Peace platform:** Students who used MartinPeaceBot in Spring 2026 know its blind spots from the inside. Their knowledge is the design material for the Fall 2026 version.

This is what the Cook-Sather (2014) SaP framework calls "reciprocal learning" — teachers and students teaching each other. It is not a nice-to-have; it is the core mechanism of all three projects.

### Conviction 4: Academic integrity is built in, not bolted on

All three platforms make a deliberate design choice: the AI cannot do the student's work, even if asked. The GTSU tutors refuse to write position papers. The career coaching prompts forbid generating finished CVs. The data journalism scaffold does not classify sources for students — it asks them to classify.

This is not a compliance gesture. It reflects a belief that the value of AI in education comes from the interaction, not the output. A student who argues with a Socratic AI tutor about whether France's position on Russia sanctions is best described as "strategic ambiguity" or "genuine mediation" has learned something. A student who pastes the AI's draft position paper into their submission has learned nothing and has produced nothing of their own.

### Conviction 5: The prompt library, not the platform, is the real deliverable

All three projects are designed to be **platform-agnostic at the output level**. The career coaching playbook works with ChatGPT or Claude or DeepSeek. The data journalism question bank works in any conversational AI. The GTSU tutor principles could be deployed on any tool.

This reflects Simon's SmartTextbook ethos: democratising AI access means ensuring that the pedagogical design, not the tool access, is what gets shared. Expensive enterprise platforms come and go. A well-designed prompt library lasts.

## Part 4 — The Cross-Project Narrative for a Funding Body

If Simon ever needs to describe all three projects together — for a research profile, a grant application, a keynote, or a promotion portfolio — the narrative is this:

> *"These three projects share one question: what happens when AI is designed to make students think harder rather than think less? In career coaching, in data journalism, and in diplomatic simulation, we have built AI tools grounded in the same conviction: that the expert–novice gap can be closed by a machine that asks the right questions at the right moment, not by one that provides the right answers. In each case, the students who use these tools are also the students who help design them — because the experience of struggling with a tool that refuses to do your thinking for you is itself a form of expertise that teachers alone cannot access. This is what Students as Partners means in an AI age: not students beta-testing a chatbot, but students whose intellectual work and lived frustrations become the architecture of learning for the next cohort."*
