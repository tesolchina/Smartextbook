## Core Problem Statement

> "Our goal is to develop AI solutions for students who struggle to practice data journalism"

Based on analysis of two NY Times articles by student partner Bess, the team identified 5 specific gaps between expert-level and student-level data journalism analysis.

---

## The "Bess Framework" — Expert vs Student Analysis

**Bess** conducted an exemplary analysis of two NY Times articles as the benchmark:

### Article 1: Extreme Heat and Social Inequality
- **Bess's approach:** Classified data sources into Government Data / Research Data / Secondary Journalistic Data; evaluated macro-level data scope and limitations
- **What students do instead:** Say "temperature data and demographics were used" — no classification, no critique

### Article 2: Who Gets to Breathe Clean Air in New Delhi?
- **Bess's approach:** Named specific instruments (AirBeam2, PurpleAir PA-II, DustTrak II 8530, UPAS); identified collaboration between journalists and ILK Labs; recognised multi-layered data approach
- **What students do instead:** "air quality sensors were used" — no instrument names, no institutional context

## Five Student Challenges Identified

### 1. Technical Precision Deficit
Students cannot name specific data collection instruments or methodologies. They lack vocabulary for describing methods precisely and miss technical details that validate data credibility.

### 2. Systematic Classification Weakness
Students treat all sources equally without hierarchical thinking. They lack Bess's framework: Government Data → Research Data → Original/Journalistic Data.

### 3. Critical Evaluation Gap
Students describe data rather than critique it. They summarise "what the data shows" without questioning methodology, scope, limitations, or representativeness.

### 4. Multi-dimensional Analysis Inability
Students analyse one dimension at a time. Bess's layered approach (Data Sources → Methodology → Scope → Critical Assessment) is beyond their current capability.

### 5. Context-Method Connection Failure
Students cannot link data collection choices to broader research/policy goals. They miss why specific instruments were chosen for specific research questions and how technical choices affect narrative credibility.

---

## AI Solutions Proposed (draft list)

1. **Technical Vocabulary Scaffolding** — AI prompts guiding students to identify and name specific instruments
2. **Classification Templates** — Structured frameworks for organising data source types
3. **Critical Question Banks** — AI-generated questions that push students beyond description
4. **Multi-layer Analysis Guides** — Step-by-step prompts for systematic evaluation
5. **Context-Method Mapping Tools** — AI assistance connecting technical choices to research goals

**Goal:** Develop AI systems that guide students through Bess's sophisticated analytical framework systematically, addressing each challenge area with targeted support.
