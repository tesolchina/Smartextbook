## Part 1 — What This Scheme Is Really About

### 1.1 The Underlying Research Project

This award scheme is not a standalone competition. It is the **public-facing component of a three-year UGC-funded research project** (2025–2028):

> *"Exploring GenAI use in teaching and learning enhancement through a pedagogical partnership approach and collaborative showcase"*

The core team spans all six partner institutions and includes HKU, HKBU, CUHK, PolyU, HKUST, and international members from NUS Singapore and Lund University Sweden. The winning projects will be:
- Published in a special RSLEIHE Report (March 2028)
- Potentially contributing to a special issue of *Teaching, Learning and Teaching in Higher Education* (TLTHE) journal (May 2027)
- Showcased at the symposium as exemplars of the "Trio model"

**Implication:** The judges are not just evaluating projects — they are collecting evidence for a research publication. The winning project needs to be **narratable as a research case study**, not just a useful tool.

### 1.2 The "Trio Model" — The Central Concept

The scheme introduces a specific theoretical framing that all proposals must embed:

> *"A fundamental shift in how universities approach teaching: moving away from traditional top-down instruction towards a collaborative teaching model, namely the **Trio model**, where faculty, students and GenAI tools jointly reshape learning experiences."*

The Trio model has three dyadic relationships:
1. **Student ↔ GenAI** — personalised, adaptive, responsive learning; cognitive partnership
2. **Teacher ↔ GenAI** — workload relief, content generation, pedagogical AI agents
3. **Student ↔ Teacher** — the SaP (Students as Partners) tradition the scheme was built on

A winning project needs to show **all three** relationships operating simultaneously and intentionally.

### 1.3 HKU's Broader AI Philosophy

HKU's AI policy (endorsed by Senate, June 2023) is built on three pillars:

**A. AI Literacy** — demystifying AI, responsible and productive interaction  
**B. Ethical Use** — goal-oriented framework: efficiency, skills development, assessment integrity  
**C. Assessment Redesign** — "Human-Machine Partnership Assessment" as a new assessment type (Chan & Colloton, 2024)

The AI Assessment Integration Framework defines **nine assessment types**, with "Human-machine partnership assessment" at the apex — evaluating students' ability to interact, utilise, and collaborate with AI while ensuring human judgment remains fundamental.

**Implication for SmartTextbook:** The platform's BYOK model (student brings their own API key, controls their own AI) is philosophically aligned with HKU's emphasis on human agency and AI literacy — not passive AI consumption.

### 1.4 What "Students as Partners" (SaP) Requires

The SaP tradition (Cook-Sather et al., 2014) demands more than students *using* a tool. It requires:
- Students as **co-designers** of the learning experience, not recipients
- Shared **decision-making** between students and faculty
- **Reciprocal learning** — both parties learn from each other
- **Authentic roles** — students contribute skills faculty don't have

**Past RSLEIHE research finding:** Graduate students exhibited higher levels of agency; female students perceived significantly more peer support. Projects that structured genuine decision-making roles for students (not just "student user testers") received stronger evaluations.

## Part 3 — SmartTextbook's Positioning

### 3.1 How SmartTextbook Naturally Fits the Trio Model

| Trio relationship | SmartTextbook implementation |
|---|---|
| **Student ↔ GenAI** | AI Tutor (Socratic/Exam Coach/Explainer styles) grounds conversation in the specific chapter the student uploaded. The student controls depth, audience level, custom instructions. |
| **Teacher ↔ GenAI** | Teacher designs the pedagogical framing: selects source material, sets depth/audience/teaching style, writes custom system prompt instruction. GenAI generates the structured lesson. |
| **Student ↔ Teacher** | SaP dimension: students co-design what "good" lesson generation looks like, provide feedback on AI outputs, evaluate quiz quality, suggest custom instructions. This is the **co-design role** that makes it genuine SaP. |

### 3.2 Which Sub-themes SmartTextbook Aligns With

The scheme offers 8 sub-themes. SmartTextbook touches several — but **pick only 1–2** for the proposal:

| Sub-theme | Fit | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| **GenAI Tools and Pedagogy** ⭐⭐⭐ | SmartTextbook is specifically a pedagogical tool for grounded AI use | **Primary** |
| **Student AI Literacy and Digital Resilience** ⭐⭐⭐ | BYOK, prompt design, critical evaluation of AI output — all are AI literacy skills | **Primary** |
| **Assessment and Feedback** ⭐⭐ | Quizzes, chat transcripts as assessable evidence; aligns with HKU Assessment Reform | Secondary |
| **Curriculum Innovation and Programme Development** ⭐⭐ | Transforming static chapter materials into interactive lessons | Secondary |
| **Self-regulated Learning** ⭐⭐ | Student controls pace, depth, style; quiz attempt logs | Secondary |

**Recommended framing:** Lead with **"GenAI Tools and Pedagogy"** + **"Student AI Literacy and Digital Resilience"**. These are the most theoretically grounded and directly addressed by the platform.

### 3.3 The Unique Differentiator — BYOK as an AI Literacy Intervention

Most GenAI education projects give students access to a centrally-provided AI tool. SmartTextbook inverts this: students provision their own AI access, choose their provider, understand the cost, and control the data.

This is not just a technical detail — it is a **pedagogical philosophy**:
- Students who set up their own API key understand that AI has costs, providers, and policies
- Students who write custom instructions understand that AI behaviour is configurable, not fixed
- Students who see their chat transcript understand they are producing evidence, not consuming a service

This maps directly onto HKU's AI literacy framework: *"Understanding the basic principles of AI, effectively interacting with AI, making informed decisions about its use, and being mindful of social, ethical, and privacy issues."*

**Frame it this way in the proposal:** SmartTextbook teaches AI literacy not through workshops, but through the act of using the tool itself. Every interaction requires AI-literate decisions.

### 3.4 The Research Gap SmartTextbook Addresses

The scheme's introductory text cites two competing concerns:
1. GenAI enables "adaptive, responsive, and interactive learning" (Gupta et al., 2024; Bhatia et al., 2024)
2. GenAI causes "unproductive cognitive offloading" and "academic dishonesty" (Ahmedtelba et al., 2025; Bittle & El-Gayar, 2025)

SmartTextbook's design directly addresses this tension:
- It is **source-grounded**: the AI can only respond based on the chapter the student uploaded — it cannot fabricate from general knowledge
- It is **process-visible**: the lesson generation, quiz attempt log, and chat transcript are all exportable artefacts
- It is **student-directed**: the student chooses the source, controls the depth, and evaluates the output

**The proposal argument:** SmartTextbook represents a designed solution to the cognitive offloading problem — not by banning AI, but by making the learning process itself visible and requiring active student agency at every step.

## Part 4 — Draft Proposal Strategy

### Section 1 — Rationale, Purpose and Expected Outcomes (~200 words)

**The problem to name:** Most digital learning materials in Hong Kong higher education remain static — PDFs, recorded lectures, fixed slide decks. When students encounter difficult chapter content, they turn to generic AI chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude) that respond from general knowledge without grounding in the specific text. This produces fluent-sounding but unverifiable content, reinforcing the cognitive offloading problem identified in the literature (Ahmedtelba et al., 2025).

**The opportunity:** The Google "Learn Your Way" research (arXiv:2509.13348, 2025) demonstrated an 11% improvement in post-test scores when students engaged with AI-augmented textbook content using a two-stage model: AI-generated structured summaries + Socratic AI tutor. The same pedagogical architecture is now available to any educator via SmartTextbook — without requiring a Google-scale engineering team.

**Expected outcomes:**
- Students demonstrate improved engagement with assigned chapter materials (measured by quiz scores and session depth)
- Students develop measurable AI literacy skills (ability to write effective custom instructions, critically evaluate AI-generated summaries)
- Students and teacher co-produce a set of validated "lesson templates" for the course that can be shared with the wider institution

### Section 2 — Partnership (~200 words)

**Roles:**
- **Teacher (Simon Wang):** Selects source chapters, defines pedagogical goals for each lesson, writes the first-draft custom system prompt, evaluates student-generated lesson quality against learning outcomes
- **Students (2–4 HKBU students):** Upload chapter content, configure lesson parameters, interact with AI tutor, evaluate the quality of generated content, propose custom instruction improvements, document the co-design process
- **GenAI tools (SmartTextbook + [DeepSeek/ChatGPT 4o]):** Generate structured lessons (summary, glossary, mind map, quiz), power the AI tutor, produce exportable transcripts for evidence collection

**Guiding SaP principles:**
- *Reciprocity* — teacher learns what students find unclear in AI-generated summaries; students gain pedagogical insight into how course objectives are designed
- *Shared decision-making* — students have genuine veto power over lesson template designs that do not serve their learning
- *Transparency* — all AI interactions are logged and visible; no black-box AI

**The co-design object:** By end of project, the Trio produces a validated, shareable "lesson generation playbook" — a set of custom instructions, depth settings, and evaluation rubrics for specific course types that other educators can adopt.

### Section 3 — Implementation (~200 words)

**Pilot course:** [Specific course at HKBU Language Centre — e.g., an academic English reading course where students work with assigned academic chapters]

**Phases:**

*Phase 1: Co-design (July–August 2026)*
- Design Sprint participation (6 June)
- Students and teacher jointly test SmartTextbook with 3–4 sample chapters from the course
- Students write evaluation reports on generated lesson quality
- Together, refine the custom instruction set and depth parameters
- Finalise the lesson generation protocol

*Phase 2: Execution (September–October 2026)*
- Students use SmartTextbook for assigned weekly chapters (minimum 6 lessons)
- Students complete AI tutor chat sessions; transcripts exported as learning evidence
- Quiz attempt logs recorded automatically
- Weekly 15-minute reflection form completed by each student

*Phase 3: Evaluation (November–December 2026)*
- Pre-post comprehension test comparison
- Analysis of quiz score progression across the semester
- Focus group with student team to evaluate AI literacy growth
- Teacher reflection on pedagogical workload shift
- Co-authorship of written report sections (students write Methodology and Reflection sections)

### Section 4 — Evaluation (~200 words)

**Evidence to collect:**

| Measure | Method | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Chapter comprehension | Pre-test (before SmartTextbook) vs post-test (after AI tutor session) | Sep & Dec 2026 |
| AI literacy | Self-assessment rubric adapted from HKU AIED AI Literacy Framework | Pre (Sep) and post (Dec) |
| Quiz progression | SmartTextbook quiz attempt logs (score + timestamp per attempt) | Continuous Sep–Dec |
| Engagement depth | AI tutor session length, number of exchanges per session | Continuous |
| Custom instruction quality | Blind evaluation of student-written instructions by teacher | Nov 2026 |
| Student perception | Reflection forms + focus group | Dec 2026 |
| Teacher workload | Time-tracking log comparing preparation time with and without SmartTextbook | Sep–Dec |

**Theoretical framework:** The evaluation is grounded in the AI Assessment Integration Framework (Chan & Colloton, 2024) — specifically the "Human-Machine Partnership Assessment" type, which evaluates students' ability to collaborate with AI while ensuring human judgment remains fundamental.

**Evidence of SaP quality:** Students co-author the Evaluation section of the final report — their authorial voice is itself evidence that the partnership was genuine.

### Section 5 — Ensuring Success (~200 words)

**Challenge 1: Student AI anxiety and unequal prior experience**
Some students may have never set up an API key. *Mitigation:* Phase 1 includes a structured onboarding session. Students with more technical experience are paired with those who have less — building peer mentorship within the team.

**Challenge 2: API access costs (BYOK model)**
Students pay for their own API usage. *Mitigation:* DeepSeek is extremely low-cost (approximately HKD $0.10–0.50 per lesson generation). Project budget includes a small fund to cover API costs for students who cannot afford them. Usage is tracked and capped per session.

**Challenge 3: AI-generated content quality varies by subject**
Language-rich chapters (the Language Centre's domain) generate higher-quality summaries than highly technical numerical content. *Mitigation:* Pilot chapters are pre-selected from the course where quality has been validated. Students evaluate and flag low-quality outputs — this becomes a learning exercise in AI discernment.

**Challenge 4: Institutional compliance**
BYOK external API use may require HKBU ethical approval. *Mitigation:* Apply for ethics clearance by July 2026 (before Phase 2 begins). If external APIs are not approved, fall back to HKU-sanctioned ChatGPT 4o as the GenAI tool.

**Challenge 5: Student co-authorship in final report**
Students may be reluctant to write formal academic sections. *Mitigation:* Students write first drafts in their own voice; teacher revises for academic register. SmartTextbook's AI tutor is used by students to improve their own writing — the tool supports the report writing process, not just the lesson content.

## Part 6 — What Makes a Winning Proposal (From Past Editions)

Based on the 4th edition (2023, Symposium Feb 2024), where **a HKU team won Champion** under "Co-Creating the Future of Education":

1. **Genuine student agency** — the winning projects gave students real design decisions, not just implementation tasks. Students co-authored the evaluation framework, not just completed questionnaires.

2. **Institutional applicability** — proposals that described how *other* courses or institutions could adopt the approach scored higher on "potential impact."

3. **Clean evidence chain** — the strongest proposals had a direct line from research question → methodology → evidence → outcome. Judges could follow the logic without effort.

4. **Research vocabulary** — proposals that used the SaP literature correctly (not just name-dropped it) were evaluated as having stronger theoretical grounding.

5. **Specificity over generality** — "we will pilot this in CAES1000 with 4 HKBU Year 1 students" is stronger than "we will run a pilot with students." The judges know the courses, the cohort sizes, the institutional realities.
