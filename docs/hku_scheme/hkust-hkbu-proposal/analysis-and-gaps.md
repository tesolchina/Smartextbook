# Proposal Analysis — Gaps, Risks & Improvements
## RSLEIHE 2026-27: AI-Enhanced Career Coaching Partnership (HKUST × HKBU)

---

## 1. What's Already Strong

- **Cross-institutional design** — HKUST + HKBU is a genuine differentiator. The scheme is co-organised across these institutions; a cross-institutional project signals broad impact and embodies the UGC research spirit.
- **Concrete course anchor** — LANG1422 is specific. Judges can picture it. Vague proposals lose.
- **Four-phase timeline** — September Foundation → October AI Training → November Pilot → December Evaluation maps cleanly onto the scheme's Sep–Dec 2026 execution window.
- **Dual-institution student population** — 50+ participants across HKUST and HKBU gives the evaluation credibility and comparative interest.
- **Platform-agnostic insight (added Apr 16)** — The recognition that AI skills should transfer across tools, not tie students to one platform, is genuinely pedagogically sound. This should be a central argument, not an appendix.

---

## 2. Critical Gaps to Fix

### Gap 1 — Section 1 has a placeholder
> *"This section requires more input about the course as well as authentic stories about career coaching"*

This is the most urgent gap. **Section 1 is where judges decide whether to keep reading.** It needs:
- A specific, concrete story about what goes wrong without personalized coaching (e.g., "In a class of 40, a teacher can review each student's CV once — typically in a 10-minute tutorial slot — leaving no time for the iterative improvement that produces real-world quality")
- The Chinese CV context: previous course iterations did not emphasize CV editing because Chinese CVs are not typically required. But as graduates enter international workplaces, this creates a documented skills gap.
- Why AI is the right intervention NOW (not just generally useful)

### Gap 2 — "Teacher clones" framing undermines SaP
The phrase "teacher clones" in Section 1 is memorable but pedagogically problematic for this scheme. The RSLEIHE is built on **Students as Partners** — the idea that students are co-creators, not recipients. "Teacher clone" positions AI as a tool that replaces student-teacher interaction rather than one that enables genuine student agency.

**Replace with:** "teacher expertise made accessible at scale" or "pedagogical partnership at scale." The key distinction is that AI gives *students* more access to high-quality formative feedback — empowering them, not replacing their relationship with the teacher.

### Gap 3 — Students described as "end users," not co-designers
The SaP literature (Cook-Sather et al., 2014) requires students to have genuine **decision-making power** in the project design, not just be validators and data generators. The current Section 2 describes students as:
- "Feedback Partners and End Users" ← this is the weakest possible SaP framing
- "Quality Validators" ← still passive
- "Data Generators" ← students generating data *for* the teachers

**Fix:** Students need a genuine co-design role. Suggested reframe: students co-design the prompt engineering rubrics, co-develop the CV evaluation criteria, and co-author sections of the final report. These are decisions that shape the project outcomes — not just feedback on a system the teachers built.

### Gap 4 — No references cited
The scheme's own reference list is a roadmap. Not citing any of these papers signals that the team hasn't engaged with the field. Every section should anchor at least one citation.

**Minimum citations to add:**
- Ahmedtelba et al. (2025) — cognitive offloading → define the problem in Section 1
- Bhatia et al. (2024) — personalised learning → justify the AI solution in Section 1
- Cook-Sather et al. (2014) — SaP → ground Section 2
- Creely & Carabott (2025) — Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogical Model → theoretical framework in Section 3

### Gap 5 — Team name inconsistency
The proposal header says "Derun Sun (HKUST)" but the project team section says "Sylvia Sun (HKUST)." This needs to be resolved before submission — it will create confusion in the review process.

### Gap 6 — GenAI tool compliance not addressed
The scheme requires teams to "comply with their university's policies and relevant regulations." ChatGPT-4 and Claude are named as the GenAI tools, but:
- HKBU's GenAI policy needs to confirm external API use is permitted for student research projects
- HKU sanctions ChatGPT 4o (not Claude) for student use
- The proposal needs a compliance statement

**Suggested approach:** Name the GenAI tools as "ChatGPT 4o (via HKBU/HKU institutional access where applicable) and supplementary open-source alternatives for students without institutional access." This is also consistent with the platform-agnostic approach.

### Gap 7 — Word count is far over limit
The scheme requires approximately 200 words per section (1,000 total). The current draft is ~1,500 words in the proposal sections alone (not counting chat logs and supporting notes). Every section needs significant cutting.

### Gap 8 — "Evaluation Methods" section is thin on the SaP dimension
The evaluation measures learning outcomes (CV scores, interview performance) but says nothing about evaluating the **quality of the partnership itself** — which is what distinguishes RSLEIHE from a standard educational technology trial. Add:
- Student co-authorship of report sections (evidence of genuine partnership)
- Student perception of decision-making agency (adapted from SaP literature)
- Reflection on what students taught the teachers (reciprocal learning)

### Gap 9 — Platform-agnostic insight not integrated into main proposal
The April 16 chat log contains a genuinely strong insight: CV skills should be transferable across platforms because students will use different AI tools throughout their careers. This is not in the main proposal. It should anchor Section 2 (Partnership Philosophy) or Section 5 (Ensuring Success — sustainability argument).

## 4. The Strongest Version of This Proposal

The most powerful framing for this project is:

> **"Most AI career coaching tools treat students as users. This project treats them as co-designers. Students who help build a career coaching AI must understand how CVs are evaluated, what interview rubrics measure, and how AI feedback can mislead — producing deeper, more transferable career literacy than any tool could provide alone."**

This framing:
- Satisfies SaP requirements (students as co-designers)
- Addresses the platform-agnostic concern (understanding the principles, not the buttons)
- Aligns with HKU's AI literacy framework
- Differentiates from a standard "we built a chatbot and students used it" project
