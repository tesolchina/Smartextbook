# What We Believe About AI in Education
## Shared Principles Across Our RSLEIHE 2026–27 Projects

*A working document for team members of the GTSU 2026, Career Coaching, and Data Journalism projects*
*Simon Wang, Lecturer in English & Innovation Officer, Language Centre, HKBU — April 2026*

---

## The Honest Starting Point

Everything we have built could, in principle, be done without any platform at all.

A teacher who writes a careful system prompt and shares it with students who then open a free chat window with DeepSeek or ChatGPT can achieve substantially the same pedagogical outcomes as our tools. The Research Advisor in MartinPeaceBot is, at its core, a very carefully written set of instructions telling an AI how to respond to a student researching France's position on Russia sanctions. The career coaching framework is a structured rubric translated into a prompt. The data journalism scaffold is a sequence of questions that guides a student from description toward critique.

This is not a confession that our work is trivial. It is the most important thing to understand about what our work actually is.

**The platform is not the point. The design is the point.**

---

## What the Design Consists Of

When we say "the design," we mean three things that travel independently of any specific tool:

**1. The scaffolding logic** — the sequence of questions or prompts that moves a student from where they are to where they need to be. For data journalism, this means moving from "sensors were used" to naming AirBeam2, PurpleAir PA-II, DustTrak II 8530. For diplomatic simulation, this means moving from "France supports sanctions" to distinguishing France's official stated position from its actual strategic interests. For career coaching, this means moving from a generic CV to one that reflects culturally-specific professional conventions.

**2. The anti-ghostwriting principle** — the decision, built into every interaction, that the AI will not do the student's thinking. It will ask the next question. It will name what is missing. It will suggest what to investigate. It will never produce the finished position paper, the classified data sources, or the polished CV. This is not a constraint we impose reluctantly. It is the condition under which learning happens. Research confirms the risk we are designing against: a 666-participant study found a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by cognitive offloading — letting AI do the cognitive work is like letting a robot do your exercise (Gerlich, 2025).

**3. The rubric** — the explicit, articulable description of what expert performance looks like and why. Bess's data journalism analysis. The GTSU 2026 assessment criteria for position papers and speeches. The LANG1422 standards for CV quality in international workplace contexts. Without a rubric, AI scaffolding has no direction. With one, it has a north star.

These three elements — scaffolding logic, anti-ghostwriting principle, and rubric — can be written down, shared, and used by any educator with any AI tool. They will outlast any platform.

## The Trio Model: Teacher, Student, and AI as Partners

The 5th RSLEIHE Award Scheme (2026–27) is built around the **Trio Model** — "a fundamental shift in how universities approach teaching: moving away from traditional top-down instruction towards a collaborative teaching model where faculty, students, and GenAI tools jointly reshape learning experiences" (TALIC/HKU, 2026). The scheme asks teams to define the roles of all three parties and specify the partnership model or guiding principles for effective collaboration.

Our projects have a specific answer to this question, built on a mechanism we call **expert knowledge distillation**:

```
Teacher's expert knowledge
         ↓  encodes into scaffolding logic + rubric
         ↓
    AI distils and scales it
         ↓  delivers personalised questioning to each student
         ↓
Student remains the decision-maker
         ↓  forms and defends judgements; experience feeds back into design
```

None of the three parties can be removed from this chain without it collapsing.

### The Teacher's Role: Encoding Expertise into Scalable Design

The teacher brings what AI cannot generate: domain expertise, institutional context, and pedagogical intuition about where students go wrong and why. In our three projects, this expertise takes specific forms:
- *Career coaching:* Years of observing what international employers actually look for, and knowing which feedback produces genuine revision versus cosmetic editing
- *Data journalism:* Expert frameworks for evaluating research methodology, classifying data sources, and critiquing study design
- *Diplomatic simulation:* Deep understanding of what rigorous position papers require — the distinction between official stance and real strategic interest, the structure of persuasive argument

The teacher's role is not to supervise AI use. It is to **encode expertise into a design** that AI can deliver at scale. This encoding — making tacit knowledge explicit, deciding which questions to ask rather than which answers to give, specifying what good looks like with enough precision that an AI can evaluate student work against it — is itself a sophisticated intellectual act. As Creely and Carabott (2025) argue in their Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogical Model, the teacher's value shifts from information transmission to expert framework design and ethical oversight; the role does not diminish, it becomes more precisely defined.

### The AI's Role: Personalised Delivery Without Replacing Thinking

AI enters our design as a *delivery mechanism for the teacher's questioning framework*, not as an independent agent. Every question the AI asks is a question the teacher would ask if they had unlimited time with every student. The AI cannot generate the framework — only the teacher can do that. The AI can deliver it to every student, at any hour, with unlimited patience.

This is the correct positioning of AI in higher education: not as a subject matter expert (it hallucinates facts; it lacks institutional context; it has no stake in the student's actual learning), but as a **scalable, personalised delivery layer** for human pedagogical design. The AACSB Human-Centric AI-First Framework (2025) places this squarely in its "Personalised Learning" phase: AI enables creativity, research, and prototyping, but the teacher-designed framework governs what counts as good work.

Critically, our AI is designed to bring students to the point of productive struggle — not to resolve the struggle. A randomised controlled trial found that proactive AI scaffolding (structured questions guiding students through expert frameworks, rather than passive chatbot responses) significantly outperforms reactive AI, and the comprehension gains persist beyond the intervention (Kwan et al., 2025). Proactive means the AI asks before the student requests, and asks rather than tells.

### The Student's Role: Decision-Maker and Design Intelligence

The student is not the recipient of a better teaching service. In a genuine Trio partnership (Cook-Sather, Bovill, & Felten, 2014; Bovill, 2020), the student has two roles that neither teacher nor AI can fill.

**As decision-maker:** At every stage of the scaffolded process, the student makes the intellectual judgement. Which country interest is most salient for France's position on Russia sanctions? Which source classification best describes NASA satellite data in a journalism study? Which achievement most deserves top billing on this CV? The AI cannot answer these questions. They require the student's judgement, knowledge of their own situation, and developing professional identity. The AI's job is to create conditions under which the student must form and defend a position.

**As design intelligence:** Students who use our systems are not passive research subjects — they are co-designers. In GTSU 2026, student partners will redesign the MartinPeaceBot tutoring prompts in Fall 2026 based on evidence from their own Spring 2026 experience. Their struggle and frustration are not problems to be solved by the teacher — they are information about where the design fails. This is what Cook-Sather, Bovill, and Felten (2014) mean by the three pillars of Students as Partners: **respect** (students have genuine expertise that teachers lack — knowledge of what it feels like to learn this material with this scaffold), **reciprocity** (both parties teach and learn from each other), and **shared responsibility** (both parties are accountable for the quality of the learning design).

### What Genuine Partnership Is Not

For clarity, genuine Trio partnership in our design is explicitly *not*:
- A teacher who uses AI to generate learning materials (teacher–AI dyad; students are the audience, not partners)
- A student who uses AI to produce first drafts for teacher review (student–AI dyad; teacher is the evaluator, not a partner)
- Any arrangement where AI replaces human judgement at the design stage (teacher's role) or the learning stage (student's role)

The test: could this project produce its learning outcomes if any one of the three parties were removed? In our design, the answer is no in all three cases.

## Platform Neutrality and BYOK: Why the Tool Doesn't Matter

Two architectural principles underpin all three projects and directly address the most common concern raised about AI in institutional teaching contexts: *what happens when the tool changes?*

**Bring Your Own Key (BYOK):** Students and educators supply their own API credentials, stored only in their browser and never held by any platform server. The institution accumulates no credential liability, no data residency risk, and no commercial dependency on our platform's continued operation. More importantly, this is a pedagogical decision: students who manage their own AI access — who see the cost of each session, who choose their provider, who bear responsibility for their own AI relationship — are learning something that students using a shared institutional account cannot. Understanding that AI is a service with economics, providers, and policies is an AI literacy outcome in its own right.

**Platform Neutrality:** The scaffolding prompts, rubrics, and assessment frameworks we develop work identically with DeepSeek, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any OpenAI-compatible interface. DeepSeek is our recommended default for Hong Kong users (Gemini is inaccessible from HK networks); ChatGPT 4o is the standard alternative. If an institution requires a specific approved provider, only the API endpoint changes — the pedagogical design is untouched. This matters for research validity: findings produced by platform-neutral scaffolding can be replicated by any institution with any tool.

Together, BYOK and platform neutrality express the same conviction: the learner's relationship with knowledge should not be intermediated by any single institution's commercial interests (Wang, 2026). ## How This Connects to the RSLEIHE Evaluation Framework

The 5th RSLEIHE scheme evaluates proposals on four dimensions. Our projects have considered each:

### 1. Team Roles and Partnership Model

As described above (Trio Model section), we define three non-interchangeable roles. The teacher encodes expertise; the AI scales delivery; the student remains the decision-maker and feeds experience back as design intelligence. The partnership model is grounded in the SaP literature (Cook-Sather et al., 2014; Bovill, 2020) and operationalised through the specific prompt co-design mechanisms described in each project's proposal.

### 2. Project Implementation Plan

All three projects execute within the September–December 2026 window specified by the scheme. Each follows a three-phase structure:
- **Phase 1 (Sept):** Baseline assessment + student partner recruitment + rubric co-design workshop
- **Phase 2 (Oct–Nov):** Course integration — AI scaffolding deployed with active data collection (interaction transcripts, assessment scores, student reflection logs)
- **Phase 3 (Dec):** Evaluation + student-partner redesign session + preliminary cross-project comparison

The SmartTextbook platform ([smartextbook.replit.app](https://smartextbook.replit.app)) provides the infrastructure. The pedagogical design is the deliverable.

### 3. Evidence and Evaluation

Our evaluation plan measures what the scaffolding was designed to teach — not just whether students enjoyed using it. Evidence collected across all three projects:
- **Pre/post assessment:** Rubric-scored student work before and after scaffolding introduction
- **Interaction transcripts:** The conversation logs between students and AI tutors, scored against the teacher-designed rubric
- **Student reflection logs:** Self-reported accounts of where the AI helped and where it failed to move them forward
- **Student partner redesign output:** The revised prompts produced by student partners, with rationale

The Chan and Colloton (2024) AI Assessment Integration Framework, developed at HKU, informs our approach: specifically their emphasis on **performance-based assessment** (what the student can do with the tool, not just what they know about it) and **process-based documentation** (the transcript as evidence, not just the final output).

### 4. Risk and Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **AI tool inaccessibility** (e.g., API outages, policy changes) | Platform-neutral design — identical scaffolding is deliverable via any compliant tool; BYOK eliminates single-provider dependency |
| **Unproductive cognitive offloading** | Anti-ghostwriting principle built into all prompts by design; AI cannot produce student deliverables — only questions |
| **Academic dishonesty** | Interaction transcripts provide process evidence; assessment tasks require student-specific judgements that AI answers cannot prefill |
| **Uneven student AI experience** | BYOK onboarding workshop in Phase 1; platform works with free-tier API access (DeepSeek is free-tier accessible in HK) |
| **Student partner attrition** | Partners recruited from students who have already completed the course; their involvement is post-assessment, not during graded work |
| **Cross-institutional coordination complexity** | Projects are independently implementable at each institution; cross-project comparison is an additional research output, not a dependency |

## What This Means for Our Three Projects

### GTSU 2026 — European Diplomacy and Peacemaking

MartinPeaceBot's four tutors (Research Advisor, Writing Coach, Speech Coach, Assessment Analyst) are the current expression of a pedagogical design that will survive any change in AI provider, platform update, or institutional policy. When student partners redesign the tutoring prompts in Fall 2026, they are not modifying software — they are refining a pedagogy based on evidence from their own learning experience. The platform makes this refinement visible and testable. It is not the reason the refinement matters.

The research finding we expect to produce is not "MartinPeaceBot improves diplomatic simulation outcomes." It is: *When students who have experienced a pedagogical scaffolding system redesign it based on evidence from their own learning, subsequent learners show deeper engagement with the expert analytical framework the system was built to teach.* That finding holds whether the interface is a custom platform, a shared GPT, or a prompt document on the first day of class.

### Career Coaching — LANG1422 / Cross-Institutional

The coaching system is, in its essence, a rubric translated into a conversation. The rubric — culturally-specific CV standards, industry-aligned achievement framing, international workplace conventions — is the asset. The AI tool delivers the rubric as a dialogue rather than a checklist, because a dialogue can respond to what a specific student has actually written. The student partners who co-design the rubric with us are doing the important work. They are deciding what counts as expert CV writing in their professional contexts. The platform makes that decision reproducible for forty students who cannot each sit with an expert coach.

### Data Journalism — Analytical Scaffolding

Bess can identify AirBeam2, PurpleAir PA-II, and DustTrak II 8530 in a New York Times data journalism article. Most students cannot. The gap is not intelligence — it is a framework for looking. Our project builds an AI scaffolding system that teaches students to look the way Bess looks. That scaffolding could be delivered by a teacher asking good questions in a seminar, a printed list of analytical prompts, or a thoughtfully configured AI chat session. We choose AI because AI is infinitely patient, available at 2am before a deadline, and will not give up on a student who needs to be asked the same foundational question six different ways before it clicks.

The five-module prompt library we build is the research output. Not the platform.

## What We Are Trying to Publish

Across our three projects, we are generating evidence for a shared research claim that no single project could substantiate alone:

> *Proactive AI scaffolding grounded in expert-derived rubrics, designed on anti-ghostwriting principles, and refined through genuine student partnership produces measurable improvements in the depth and specificity of student analytical work — regardless of discipline, subject matter, or AI provider.*

Each project is a different disciplinary test:
- **Diplomatic simulation** (GTSU 2026): research literacy + argumentation + oral rhetoric
- **Career coaching** (LANG1422): professional writing + cultural contextualisation
- **Data journalism**: source evaluation + methodological critique + technical vocabulary

The cross-project comparison is the paper. The individual projects are the data. The platform-neutral design is what makes the finding generalisable beyond any single institution or tool.

---

## A Note on What "Platform-Neutral" Means for You

If you are a teacher or student partner on one of these projects, "platform-neutral" means one concrete thing: the work you do — co-designing prompts, refining rubrics, writing evaluations — is yours. It does not belong to any AI company. It does not require any platform subscription to remain useful. If tomorrow every AI company changed its pricing model, your prompt library would still work with whatever tool remained accessible.

You are not being asked to learn a tool. You are being asked to develop a design practice. That practice — asking better questions, specifying what expert performance looks like, building feedback loops that develop skill rather than bypass it — will serve you in every teaching and learning context you encounter for the rest of your career, regardless of what AI looks like in five years.

That is the point.
