# What We Believe About AI in Education
## Shared Principles Across Our RSLEIHE Projects

*A working document for team members of the GTSU 2026, Career Coaching, and Data Journalism projects*
*Simon Wang, Language Centre, HKBU — April 2026*

---

## The Honest Starting Point

Everything we have built could, in principle, be done without any platform at all.

A teacher who writes a careful system prompt and shares it with students who then open a free chat window with DeepSeek or ChatGPT can achieve substantially the same pedagogical outcomes as our tools. The Research Advisor in MartinPeaceBot is, at its core, a very carefully written set of instructions telling an AI how to respond to a student researching France's position on Russia sanctions. The career coaching framework is a structured rubric translated into a prompt. The data journalism scaffold is a sequence of questions that guide a student from description toward critique.

This is not a confession that our work is trivial. It is the most important thing to understand about what our work actually is.

**The platform is not the point. The design is the point.**

---

## What the Design Consists Of

When we say "the design," we mean three things that travel independently of any specific tool:

**1. The scaffolding logic** — the sequence of questions or prompts that moves a student from where they are to where they need to be. For data journalism, this means moving from "sensors were used" to naming AirBeam2, PurpleAir PA-II, DustTrak II 8530. For diplomatic simulation, this means moving from "France supports sanctions" to distinguishing France's official stated position from its actual strategic interests. For career coaching, this means moving from a generic CV to one that reflects culturally-specific professional conventions.

**2. The anti-ghostwriting principle** — the decision, built into every interaction, that the AI will not do the student's thinking. It will ask the next question. It will name what is missing. It will suggest what to investigate. It will never produce the finished position paper, the classified data sources, or the polished CV. This is not a constraint we impose reluctantly. It is the condition under which learning happens.

**3. The rubric** — the explicit, articulable description of what expert performance looks like and why. Bess's data journalism analysis. The GTSU 2026 assessment criteria for position papers and speeches. The LANG1422 standards for CV quality in international workplace contexts. Without a rubric, AI scaffolding has no direction. With one, it has a north star.

These three elements — the scaffolding logic, the anti-ghostwriting principle, and the rubric — can be written down, shared, and used by any educator with any AI tool. They will outlast any platform.

---

## So Why Build Anything?

If the design is what matters and the design can live in a document, why build tools at all?

There are three honest answers.

**Reproducibility.** A prompt library that exists only in one teacher's head, or in a Google Doc that students may or may not read, does not produce consistent learning experiences. When the scaffolding is embedded in a structured interface, every student encounters the same thoughtfully designed sequence, regardless of whether they are in Hong Kong or Macau, on a laptop or a phone, in week two or week ten of the semester. Reproducibility is the condition for research that means something.

**Evidence.** A student who writes a good position paper proves they can write a good position paper. A student whose chat transcript shows fourteen exchanges with a Research Advisor — each one pushing them to specify more precisely, to distinguish official from actual, to find a primary source — proves something more interesting: they can think through a complex problem with a tool that refuses to think for them. The transcript is the evidence. Without a structured platform, that evidence evaporates.

**Scale.** A teacher who can give ten minutes of individual coaching per student per semester cannot, alone, give every student the iterative, responsive feedback that produces genuine skill development. AI scaffolding that is thoughtfully designed can provide something closer to that experience for every student. The platform makes this feasible at course scale. The design makes it pedagogically sound.

## What This Means for Our Three Projects

### GTSU 2026 — European Diplomacy and Peacemaking

MartinPeaceBot's four tutors are the current expression of a pedagogical design that will survive any change in AI provider, any platform update, and any shift in institutional policy on approved tools. When student partners redesign the tutoring prompts in Fall 2026, they are not modifying software — they are refining a pedagogy based on evidence from their own learning experience. The platform makes this refinement visible and testable. It is not the reason the refinement matters.

The research finding we expect to produce is not "MartinPeaceBot improves diplomatic simulation outcomes." It is: "When students who have experienced a pedagogical scaffolding system redesign it based on evidence from their own learning, subsequent learners show deeper engagement with the expert analytical framework the system was built to teach." That finding holds whether the interface is a custom platform, a shared GPT, or a prompt document handed to students on the first day of class.

### Career Coaching — LANG1422 / HKUST Cross-Institutional

The coaching system we are building is, in its essence, a rubric translated into a conversation. The rubric — culturally-specific CV standards, industry-aligned achievement framing, international workplace conventions — is the asset. The AI tool delivers the rubric as a dialogue rather than a checklist, because a dialogue can respond to what a specific student has actually written, not what a generic student might write. The student partners who co-design the rubric with us are doing the important work. They are deciding what counts as expert CV writing in their professional contexts. The platform is how we make that decision reproducible for forty students who cannot each sit with an expert coach.

### Data Journalism — Analytical Scaffolding

Bess can identify AirBeam2 and PurpleAir PA-II and DustTrak II 8530 in a New York Times data journalism article. Most students cannot. The gap between Bess and a typical student is not intelligence — it is a framework for looking. Our project builds an AI scaffolding system that teaches students to look the way Bess looks. That scaffolding could be delivered by a teacher asking good questions in a seminar. It could be delivered by a printed list of analytical prompts. It could be delivered by a thoughtfully configured AI chat session. We choose AI because AI is infinitely patient, available at 2am before a deadline, and will not give up on a student who needs to be asked the same foundational question six different ways before it clicks.

The five-module prompt library we build is the research output. Not the platform.

## How This Connects to What HKU Is Looking For

The RSLEIHE scheme asks for evidence of learning experience redesign — not technology deployment. It asks how students become genuine partners in shaping their own education — not how institutions licence AI tools. It asks what is replicable, transferable, and sustainable beyond a single trial — not what is impressive in a demonstration.

Our answer to all three questions is the same: **the pedagogical design.**

The design is replicable because it is documented as a prompt library and a rubric — not locked inside a platform subscription.

The design is transferable because it is built on BYOK and platform-neutral principles — any institution with any AI provider can adopt it without commercial negotiation.

The design is sustainable because its central mechanism is student partnership — the students who use it become the students who improve it for the next cohort. No budget line is required to sustain a community of practice.

The platform is the vehicle. The design is the destination.

## What We Are Trying to Publish

Across our three projects, we are generating evidence for a shared research claim that no single project could substantiate alone:

> *Proactive AI scaffolding grounded in expert-derived rubrics, designed on anti-ghostwriting principles, and refined through student partnership produces measurable improvements in the depth and specificity of student analytical work — regardless of discipline, subject matter, or AI provider.*

Each project is a different disciplinary test of this claim:
- Diplomatic simulation (GTSU 2026): research literacy + argumentation + oral rhetoric
- Career coaching (LANG1422): professional writing + cultural contextualisation
- Data journalism: source evaluation + methodological critique + technical vocabulary

The cross-project comparison is the paper. The individual projects are the data.

If this framing resonates with you as a team member, it has a practical implication: the evidence we collect matters only if it is consistent, comparable, and honest about what it does and does not prove. This means: careful rubric design before the semester starts, baseline assessment before the AI scaffolding is introduced, and evaluation that measures what the scaffolding was designed to teach — not just whether students enjoyed using it.

---

## A Note on What "Platform-Neutral" Means for You

If you are a teacher or student partner on one of these projects, "platform-neutral" means one concrete thing: the work you do — co-designing prompts, refining rubrics, writing evaluations — is yours. It does not belong to any AI company. It does not require any platform subscription to remain useful. If tomorrow every AI company changed its pricing model, your prompt library would still work with whatever tool remained accessible. If our platforms shut down, your pedagogical design would still be expressible in any system-prompt interface.

You are not being asked to learn a tool. You are being asked to develop a design practice. That practice — asking better questions, specifying what expert performance looks like, building feedback loops that develop skill rather than bypass it — will serve you in every teaching and learning context you encounter for the rest of your career, regardless of what AI looks like in five years.

That is the point.
