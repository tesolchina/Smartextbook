# What We Believe About AI in Education
## Shared Principles Across Our RSLEIHE Projects

*A working document for team members of the GTSU 2026, Career Coaching, and Data Journalism projects*
*Simon Wang, Lecturer in English & Innovation Officer, Language Centre, HKBU — April 2026*

---

## The Honest Starting Point

Everything we have built could, in principle, be done without any platform at all.

A teacher who writes a careful system prompt and shares it with students who then open a free chat window with DeepSeek or ChatGPT can achieve substantially the same pedagogical outcomes as our tools. The Research Advisor in MartinPeaceBot is, at its core, a very carefully written set of instructions telling an AI how to respond to a student researching France's position on Russia sanctions. The career coaching framework is a structured rubric translated into a prompt. The data journalism scaffold is a sequence of questions that guide a student from description toward critique.

This is not a confession that our work is trivial. It is the most important thing to understand about what our work actually is.

**The platform is not the point. The design is the point.**

---

## What the Design Consists Of

When we say "the design," we mean three things that travel independently of any specific tool:

**1. The scaffolding logic** — the sequence of questions or prompts that moves a student from where they are to where they need to be. For data journalism, this means moving from "sensors were used" to naming AirBeam2, PurpleAir PA-II, DustTrak II 8530. For diplomatic simulation, this means moving from "France supports sanctions" to distinguishing France's official stated position from its actual strategic interests. For career coaching, this means moving from a generic CV to one that reflects culturally-specific professional conventions.

**2. The anti-ghostwriting principle** — the decision, built into every interaction, that the AI will not do the student's thinking. It will ask the next question. It will name what is missing. It will suggest what to investigate. It will never produce the finished position paper, the classified data sources, or the polished CV. This is not a constraint we impose reluctantly. It is the condition under which learning happens. Research confirms the risk: a 666-participant study found a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by cognitive offloading — the "gym workout" problem, where letting AI do the work is like letting a robot do your exercise (Gerlich, 2025).

**3. The rubric** — the explicit, articulable description of what expert performance looks like and why. Bess's data journalism analysis. The GTSU 2026 assessment criteria for position papers and speeches. The LANG1422 standards for CV quality in international workplace contexts. Without a rubric, AI scaffolding has no direction. With one, it has a north star.

These three elements — the scaffolding logic, the anti-ghostwriting principle, and the rubric — can be written down, shared, and used by any educator with any AI tool. They will outlast any platform.

## Three-Way Partnership: How Teacher, Student, and AI Collaborate

The RSLEIHE scheme asks for genuine three-way collaboration — Trio partnership — between teachers, students, and GenAI (TALIC/HKU, 2026). This framing matters because it is easy to have all three parties present without any genuine partnership occurring. A teacher who deploys AI to give students feedback has not created a Trio — they have replaced one part of a dyad with a faster version. A student who uses AI to generate a draft has not partnered with AI — they have outsourced.

What genuine three-way partnership means in our projects is best described through a mechanism we call **expert knowledge distillation**:

```
Teacher's expert knowledge
         ↓
   AI distils and scales it
         ↓
Every student gets personalised access
to the questioning framework — not the answers
```

Each party has a distinct and irreplaceable role:

### The Teacher's Role: Setting Direction and Encoding Expertise

The teacher brings what AI cannot generate: deep domain expertise, an understanding of the specific institutional context, and accumulated pedagogical intuition about where students go wrong and why. In our projects, this expertise takes several forms:
- *In career coaching:* Years of observing CV patterns, understanding what international employers actually look for, and knowing which feedback produces genuine revision versus cosmetic editing
- *In data journalism:* Expert analytical frameworks for evaluating methodology, classifying sources, and critiquing research design
- *In diplomatic simulation:* Deep understanding of what rigorous position papers require — the distinction between official stance and real strategic interest, the structure of persuasive diplomatic argument

The teacher's role is not to supervise AI use. It is to encode their expertise into a design that AI can deliver at scale. This encoding is itself a sophisticated intellectual act: it requires making tacit knowledge explicit, deciding what questions to ask rather than what answers to give, and specifying what good looks like with enough precision that an AI can evaluate student work against it.

This connects to what Creely and Carabott (2025) call the "Positionality of the Teacher" in their Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogical Model: the teacher's value shifts from information transmission to expert framework design and ethical oversight. The teacher does not become less important — their role becomes more precisely defined.

### The AI's Role: Scaling Personalised Delivery Without Replacing Thinking

AI enters the design as a *delivery mechanism for the teacher's questioning framework*, not as an independent agent. Every question the AI asks in our systems is a question the teacher would ask if they had unlimited time with every student. The AI cannot generate the framework — only the teacher can do that. The AI can deliver it to every student, in every session, at any hour, with unlimited patience.

This is the correct positioning of AI in higher education: not as a subject matter expert (it halluccinates facts; it lacks institutional context; it has no stake in the student's actual learning), but as a *scalable, personalised delivery layer* for human pedagogical design.

The AACSB Human-Centric AI-First Framework (2025) describes this as the Personalised Learning phase of the Trio: students use GenAI for creativity, research, prototyping, and peer formation — but the teacher-designed framework governs what counts as good work. The AI provides the conversation; the teacher provides the criteria.

Critically, AI in our projects is designed to bring students to the point of productive struggle — not to resolve the struggle. A 2025 randomised controlled trial found that proactive AI scaffolding (structured questions guiding students through expert frameworks) significantly outperforms passive chatbots, and the comprehension gains persist beyond the intervention (Kwan et al., 2025). The key word is *proactive*: the AI asks before the student requests, and asks rather than tells.

### The Student's Role: Decision-Maker and Knowledge Contributor

The student is not the recipient of a better teaching service. In a genuine Trio partnership, the student has two roles that neither the teacher nor AI can fill.

**As decision-maker:** At every stage of the scaffolded process, the student makes the intellectual judgement. Which country interest is most salient for France's position on Russia sanctions? Which data source classification best describes NASA satellite data in a journalism study? Which achievement most deserves top billing on this CV for this industry? The AI cannot answer these questions — they require the student's judgement, knowledge of their own situation, and developing professional identity. The AI can only create the conditions under which the student must form and defend a judgement.

**As knowledge contributor:** The students who use our systems are not passive research subjects. They are co-designers — in some cases literally (GTSU 2026 student partners redesigning the tutor prompts), in others structurally (Bess's expert analytical framework becoming the scaffold for future students). This is what Cook-Sather, Bovill, and Felten (2014) mean by the three pillars of Students as Partners: *respect* (students have genuine expertise that teachers lack), *reciprocity* (both parties teach and learn from each other), and *shared responsibility* (both parties are accountable for the quality of the learning design).

The students who struggled with MartinPeaceBot's Research Advisor in Spring 2026 know something about how it fails that the teacher who designed it cannot access. That knowledge is not anecdote — it is design intelligence. The RSLEIHE project makes it useful.

### What Three-Way Partnership Is Not

For clarity, three-way partnership in our design is explicitly *not*:
- A teacher who uses AI to generate learning materials (this is a teacher-AI dyad; students are the audience, not partners)
- A student who uses AI to produce first drafts for teacher review (this is a student-AI dyad; the teacher is the evaluator, not a partner)
- Any arrangement where AI replaces human judgement at either the design stage (teacher's role) or the learning stage (student's role)

The test of genuine Trio partnership is simple: could this project produce its learning outcomes if any one of the three parties were removed? In our design, the answer is no in all three cases. Remove the teacher, and there is no expert framework to distil. Remove the AI, and the framework cannot reach every student personally. Remove the student as an active intellectual agent, and there is no learning — only output.

## So Why Build Anything?

If the design is what matters and the design can live in a document, why build tools at all?

There are three honest answers.

**Reproducibility.** A prompt library that exists only in one teacher's head, or in a Google Doc that students may or may not read, does not produce consistent learning experiences. When the scaffolding is embedded in a structured interface, every student encounters the same thoughtfully designed sequence, regardless of whether they are in Hong Kong or Macau, on a laptop or a phone, in week two or week ten of the semester. Reproducibility is the condition for research that means something.

**Evidence.** A student who writes a good position paper proves they can write a good position paper. A student whose chat transcript shows fourteen exchanges with a Research Advisor — each one pushing them to specify more precisely, to distinguish official from actual, to find a primary source — proves something more interesting: they can think through a complex problem with a tool that refuses to think for them. The transcript is the evidence. Without a structured platform, that evidence evaporates.

**Scale.** A teacher who can give ten minutes of individual coaching per student per semester cannot, alone, give every student the iterative, responsive feedback that produces genuine skill development. AI scaffolding that is thoughtfully designed can provide something closer to that experience for every student. The platform makes this feasible at course scale. The design makes it pedagogically sound.

## Platform Neutrality and BYOK: Why the Tool Doesn't Matter

Two architectural principles underpin all three projects and directly address the most common concern raised about AI in institutional teaching contexts: *what happens when the tool changes?*

**Bring Your Own Key (BYOK):** Students and educators supply their own API credentials, stored only in their browser and never held by any platform server. The institution accumulates no credential liability, no data residency risk, and no commercial dependency on our platform's continued operation. More importantly, this is a pedagogical decision: students who manage their own AI access — who see the cost of each session, who choose their provider, who bear responsibility for their own AI relationship — are learning something that students using a shared institutional account cannot. Understanding that AI is a service with economics, providers, and policies is an AI literacy outcome in its own right. BYOK teaches this through the act of using the tool.

**Platform Neutrality:** The scaffolding prompts, rubrics, and assessment frameworks we develop work identically with DeepSeek, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any OpenAI-compatible interface. DeepSeek is our recommended default for Hong Kong users given its accessibility; ChatGPT 4o is the standard alternative. If an institution requires a specific approved provider, only the API endpoint changes — the pedagogical design is untouched. This matters for research validity: findings produced by platform-neutral scaffolding can be replicated by any institution with any tool. Findings produced by platform-specific features cannot.

Together, BYOK and platform neutrality express the same conviction: the learner's relationship with knowledge should not be intermediated by any single institution's commercial interests (Wang, 2026). These principles are documented in detail in the project's technical design document ([DESIGN_PRINCIPLES.md](https://github.com/tesolchina/Smartextbook/blob/master/DESIGN_PRINCIPLES.md)).

## What This Means for Our Three Projects

### GTSU 2026 — European Diplomacy and Peacemaking

MartinPeaceBot's four tutors are the current expression of a pedagogical design that will survive any change in AI provider, any platform update, and any shift in institutional policy on approved tools. When student partners redesign the tutoring prompts in Fall 2026, they are not modifying software — they are refining a pedagogy based on evidence from their own learning experience. The platform makes this refinement visible and testable. It is not the reason the refinement matters.

The research finding we expect to produce is not "MartinPeaceBot improves diplomatic simulation outcomes." It is: "When students who have experienced a pedagogical scaffolding system redesign it based on evidence from their own learning, subsequent learners show deeper engagement with the expert analytical framework the system was built to teach." That finding holds whether the interface is a custom platform, a shared GPT, or a prompt document handed to students on the first day of class.

### Career Coaching — LANG1422 / Cross-Institutional

The coaching system we are building is, in its essence, a rubric translated into a conversation. The rubric — culturally-specific CV standards, industry-aligned achievement framing, international workplace conventions — is the asset. The AI tool delivers the rubric as a dialogue rather than a checklist, because a dialogue can respond to what a specific student has actually written, not what a generic student might write. The student partners who co-design the rubric with us are doing the important work. They are deciding what counts as expert CV writing in their professional contexts. The platform is how we make that decision reproducible for forty students who cannot each sit with an expert coach.

### Data Journalism — Analytical Scaffolding

Bess can identify AirBeam2 and PurpleAir PA-II and DustTrak II 8530 in a New York Times data journalism article. Most students cannot. The gap between Bess and a typical student is not intelligence — it is a framework for looking. Our project builds an AI scaffolding system that teaches students to look the way Bess looks. That scaffolding could be delivered by a teacher asking good questions in a seminar. It could be delivered by a printed list of analytical prompts. It could be delivered by a thoughtfully configured AI chat session. We choose AI because AI is infinitely patient, available at 2am before a deadline, and will not give up on a student who needs to be asked the same foundational question six different ways before it clicks.

The five-module prompt library we build is the research output. Not the platform.

## How This Connects to What HKU Is Looking For

The 5th RSLEIHE Award Scheme (2026–27) is co-organised by HKU, HKBU, CUHK, PolyU, and HKUST, funded through the UGC Inter-Institutional Collaborative Project on "Exploring GenAI use in teaching and learning enhancement through a pedagogical partnership approach." Its central concern — stated explicitly in the scheme documentation — is the risk of *unproductive cognitive offloading* and *heightened academic dishonesty*, balanced against the genuine opportunity AI offers for adaptive, responsive, and interactive learning (TALIC/HKU, 2026).

Our projects address both sides of this concern through design, not through policy:
- The anti-ghostwriting principle ensures AI cannot produce the student's work, only the questions that move the student toward it
- The Trio model ensures teacher expertise governs what the AI delivers, not the AI's own pattern-matching defaults
- The student-as-co-designer structure ensures the scaffolding is refined by people who know, from experience, where it fails

The RSLEIHE scheme asks for evidence of learning experience redesign — not technology deployment. It asks how students become genuine partners in shaping their own education — not how institutions licence AI tools. It asks what is replicable, transferable, and sustainable beyond a single trial — not what is impressive in a demonstration.

Our answer to all three questions is the same: **the pedagogical design.**

The design is replicable because it is documented as a prompt library and a rubric — not locked inside a platform subscription.

The design is transferable because it is built on BYOK and platform-neutral principles — any institution with any AI provider can adopt it without commercial negotiation.

The design is sustainable because its central mechanism is student partnership — the students who use it become the students who improve it for the next cohort. No budget line is required to sustain a community of practice.

The platform is the vehicle. The design is the destination.

## What We Are Trying to Publish

Across our three projects, we are generating evidence for a shared research claim that no single project could substantiate alone:

> *Proactive AI scaffolding grounded in expert-derived rubrics, co-designed through genuine student partnership, and built on platform-neutral principles produces measurable improvements in the depth and specificity of student analytical work — across disciplines, subject matters, and AI providers.*

Each project is a different disciplinary test of this claim:
- Diplomatic simulation (GTSU 2026): research literacy + argumentation + oral rhetoric
- Career coaching: professional writing + cultural contextualisation
- Data journalism: source evaluation + methodological critique + technical vocabulary

The cross-project comparison is the paper. The individual projects are the data. The platform-neutral design is what makes the finding generalisable.

If this framing resonates with you as a team member, it has a practical implication: the evidence we collect matters only if it is consistent, comparable, and honest about what it does and does not prove. This means: careful rubric design before the semester starts, baseline assessment before AI scaffolding is introduced, and evaluation that measures what the scaffolding was designed to teach — not just whether students enjoyed using it.

---

## A Note on What "Platform-Neutral" Means for You

If you are a teacher or student partner on one of these projects, "platform-neutral" means one concrete thing: the work you do — co-designing prompts, refining rubrics, writing evaluations — is yours. It does not belong to any AI company. It does not require any platform subscription to remain useful. If tomorrow every AI company changed its pricing model, your prompt library would still work with whatever tool remained accessible. If our platforms shut down, your pedagogical design would still be expressible in any system-prompt interface.

You are not being asked to learn a tool. You are being asked to develop a design practice. That practice — asking better questions, specifying what expert performance looks like, building feedback loops that develop skill rather than bypass it — will serve you in every teaching and learning context you encounter for the rest of your career, regardless of what AI looks like in five years.

That is the point.
