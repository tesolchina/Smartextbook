## Introduction

### What This Case Is About

*(Target: 100 words)*

This teaching case reports on a seven-session workshop programme — the Generative AI Agent Student Ambassador Programme (genAI2026) — conducted at the Language Centre of Hong Kong Baptist University (HKBU) in early 2026. The programme taught non-programming-background students to use AI agent tools (Trae IDE, Cursor) to transform static academic articles into interactive digital learning modules, without writing conventional code. Participants communicated their design intentions to an AI assistant in natural language — a practice increasingly termed "natural language programming" or "vibe coding" — and the AI agent produced the corresponding software artifacts.

### Why This Case Is Relevant to TPC Readers

*(Target: 100 words)*

Professional communicators are increasingly expected to work alongside generative AI tools to produce interactive, multimodal content. Yet most practical guidance assumes programming literacy. This case addresses a critical gap: how can professional communication educators equip learners who have no programming background to leverage the full capability of agentic AI tools? The case contributes a replicable, Bring-Your-Own-Key (BYOK) pedagogical model that is transferable across institutional contexts and free from vendor lock-in — directly relevant to the *Transactions'* audience of educators, practitioners, and researchers in technical and professional communication.

### Research Questions

This teaching case was guided by three research questions:

1. How do students without programming backgrounds experience using AI agent tools in an integrated development environment (IDE) for professional communication document tasks?
2. In what ways does natural language programming support computational thinking development among language and professional communication students?
3. What pedagogical challenges and opportunities emerge when integrating agentic AI tools into professional communication education?

### Organization of This Article

This article is organized as follows. *Situating the Case* reviews similar cases and the theoretical frameworks that guided this work. *How This Case Was Studied* describes the data collection and analysis methods. *About the Case* presents the problem, the solution, and the process of developing it. *Results* addresses each research question in turn. *Discussion* interprets the findings in light of the broader literature. *Conclusion* summarizes implications for practitioners and researchers.

**Case 1: AI-Augmented Textbook (Elidan et al., 2025)**  
Elidan et al. [1] describe an AI-augmented textbook system in which students interact with embedded generative AI tools to deepen engagement with static content. Unlike the present case, their model retains the traditional textbook structure and uses AI to augment comprehension rather than to produce new artifacts. However, their finding that "AI scaffolding shifts learner attention from content acquisition to content application" directly supports our pedagogical rationale.

**Case 2: Vibe Coding — Programming Through Conversation with AI (Sarkar & Drosos, 2025)**  
Sarkar and Drosos [2] coined the term "vibe coding" to describe the emerging practice of directing AI to generate code through natural language description. Their analysis of professional programmers using AI agents in VS Code provides a baseline for understanding what experienced users do — a useful contrast for our study of novice, non-programming learners who must develop both the communication skill and the computational mental model simultaneously.

**Case 3: Writing Is Coding (Lin, 2025)**  
Lin [3] argues that document production in professional communication contexts shares structural properties with programming: both involve specification, iteration, and debugging. This conceptual alignment is central to our pedagogical approach, which treats natural language instructions to an AI agent as a form of low-barrier coding.

### Participants

Participants were undergraduate and postgraduate students at HKBU enrolled in language or communication-related programs. The programme was open to any student who wished to join the Ambassador cohort; participants were self-selected volunteers. Approximately 50–60 students attended each of the seven sessions.

### Data Collection

Data were collected across seven workshop sessions held via Zoom between February and March 2026. The following data sources were used:

- **Session recordings** (Zoom): full recording of each session with screen share
- **Participant-produced artifacts**: the interactive HTML modules created by participants during sessions
- **Exit surveys**: 5-item Likert-scale questionnaire + 2 open-ended questions administered after each session
- **Instructor field notes**: written within 24 hours of each session
- **Informal interviews**: brief post-session conversations with 8–10 volunteer participants

### The Problem

**Basic problem as originally presented:**  

Professional communication students at HKBU's Language Centre had limited exposure to generative AI tools beyond simple text chatbots (e.g., ChatGPT, Poe). The existing curriculum treated AI as a writing assistant — a tool to improve drafts — rather than as an agent capable of producing complex technical artifacts (interactive web pages, structured datasets, learning modules) from natural language instructions. As a result, students were unprepared for the rapidly evolving professional landscape in which agentic AI is transforming document production workflows.

**Major constraints:**

| Constraint | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| No programming background | Participants were language and communication students with minimal or no prior coding experience |
| No institutional AI budget | The university did not provide AI API access to students; each student needed their own API key |
| No dedicated lab | Sessions were held online (Zoom); participants used their own devices (BYOD) |
| Short intervention window | 7 sessions × ~90 minutes each, with student attendance varying across sessions |
| Ethical constraints | No compulsory participation; student artifacts required consent for research use |

### The Solution

#### Brief Description

*(75–125 words)*

The genAI2026 programme delivered seven online workshops using the **PITA framework** — Prepare, Interact, Transform, Assess — as a pedagogical scaffolding model. Participants were guided to choose a published professional communication article, extract its key arguments using structured prompts, and direct an AI agent (Trae IDE or Cursor) to convert the article into an interactive HTML learning module. The intended audience was fellow students and professional communication practitioners who would encounter the article's topic in their careers. All tools used were open-source or BYOK-compatible, requiring no institutional license.

#### Walk-Through of the Solution

*(200–300 words — describe the final deliverable from start to finish)*

The solution — a complete interactive learning module — is illustrated by the *Listening as Engineering Communication* demo module (hereafter "the Listening Demo"), created as a reference implementation during the programme. The module begins with a header section identifying the source article (Leydens & Lucena, 2009), followed by sequential content sections corresponding to the article's main arguments. Each section contains:

1. **AI-narrated audio** (generated via ElevenLabs text-to-speech, using corrected phonetics provided by the instructor)  
2. **Highlighted key phrases** — visually emphasized inline text corresponding to core concepts  
3. **Concept tags** — clickable labels for key themes (e.g., "Active Listening," "Stakeholder Needs")  
4. **Design Commentary button** — a sidebar toggle revealing the instructor's pedagogical rationale for each design choice  
5. **Reflective prompts** — questions embedded at section breaks to prompt critical engagement  
6. **Progress tracking** — completion indicators visible to the learner  

The module is served as a static HTML file with no server-side dependencies, making it deployable without institutional infrastructure. A live version is accessible at: https://smartextbook.replit.app/listening-demo.html

### RQ1: Experiencing AI Agent Tools in an IDE

*(100 words max per finding, supported by participant quotes)*

*[Action: Fill in from exit survey + interview data]*

Preliminary themes from session observations and participant feedback suggest:
- Initial **overwhelm** with the IDE interface, followed by rapid **acclimation** once the chat-based interface was identified as the primary interaction mode
- Participants described the experience as "talking to a very fast intern" — indicating a shift in mental model from AI-as-editor to AI-as-producer
- Students with document design experience (desktop publishing, graphic design) adapted fastest

### RQ2: Natural Language Programming and Computational Thinking

*[Action: Fill in from observations and artifacts]*

Emergent themes:
- Participants developed **specificity** in their prompts over time — a form of computational precision without formal syntax
- Debugging in natural language ("the button doesn't appear on mobile, fix it") mirrored the debugging practice of conventional programmers
- Students described learning to "think like a designer who can describe exactly what they want" — aligned with Wing's (2006) definition of computational thinking as problem decomposition and abstraction

### RQ3: Pedagogical Challenges and Opportunities

*[Action: Fill in — specific challenges observed]*

Challenges:
- **API key setup** was the most significant access barrier; approximately 20% of participants could not complete setup during the session
- **Context window limitations** required students to learn to break large tasks into smaller prompts — a non-intuitive skill for first-time users
- **Quality assessment** was difficult for participants who could not read the generated HTML code

Opportunities:
- **Learnersourcing effect**: participants who transformed an article reported deeper comprehension of the source content than they expected
- **Portfolio artifact**: the completed module served as a demonstrable professional skill artifact for participants' career portfolios
- **Transferability**: participants applied the PITA framework to non-academic documents (corporate reports, policy documents) in follow-up sessions

## Conclusion

### Summary of Findings

*[3–4 sentences summarizing the main takeaways from each RQ]*

### Implications for Practitioners

The genAI2026 case offers several lessons for professional communication educators:

1. **BYOK is viable at scale** — students can obtain and manage their own AI API keys with structured guidance, reducing dependency on institutional AI budgets.
2. **IDE environments are not inherently exclusionary** — with appropriate scaffolding (PITA framework), non-programming students can use agent-mode IDEs productively within a single session.
3. **The output is the pedagogy** — when students produce real, publishable artifacts rather than exercises, engagement and depth of learning increase.

### Limitations

- The sample was self-selected volunteers, limiting generalizability
- Data collection was informal for early sessions (laissez-faire) due to ethics approval timeline
- The programme was conducted entirely online, limiting observation of peer interaction
- Long-term skill retention was not assessed

### Future Research

Future studies should examine:
- Whether BYOK-based agentic AI skill acquisition transfers to workplace document production tasks
- The role of xAPI learning analytics in providing formative feedback during natural language programming sessions
- Longitudinal effects of learnersourcing participation on professional communication competency
