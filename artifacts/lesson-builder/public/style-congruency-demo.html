Section 1 of 3 — Research Question

Why Writing Style Matters Internationally

As businesses expand globally, a pressing question emerges for professional communicators: should you adapt the writing style of your documents to suit different cultural audiences?


There are two schools of thought:

🌐 Standardization

Use the same style everywhere. Globalization erodes cultural differences — a uniform message is simpler and cheaper.

📍 Localization

Adapt style to match each culture's preferences. People respond better to messages that feel familiar and congruent with their values.

The concept at the heart of this debate is style congruency — the idea that a writing style aligned with the cultural preferences of the target audience will be more persuasive than one that is not.


Key terms: Style congruency means writing in a way that matches the stylistic preferences of a particular culture. If it works, organizations should localize. If it doesn't, one global style is fine — and cheaper.

Quick Check 1

In this research context, what does "style congruency" mean?

✓ Correct! Style congruency means adapting your writing style to fit the preferences of a specific cultural audience — for example, using a more elaborate style in cultures that prefer it. The research question is whether this adaptation actually makes your message more persuasive.
Not quite. Style congruency is specifically about cultural alignment — adapting to the stylistic preferences of a particular national or cultural group. It is not about standardization (which is the opposite) or about matching content type.
Section 2 of 3 — Study Design

The Study: Two Dimensions, Two Countries

Hendriks and colleagues designed a quantitative experimental study with 344 business-to-business customers in the Netherlands and Great Britain. Participants read different versions of a newsletter and rated it on three scales:

  • Comprehensibility — how easy was it to understand?
  • Attractiveness — how appealing was the newsletter?
  • Purchase intention — how likely were they to order goods?

They manipulated two writing style dimensions:

📏 Dimension 1: Conciseness

Concise — short sentences, minimal elaboration, direct points


Wordy — longer sentences, more elaboration, indirect phrasing

🎯 Dimension 2: Orientation

Sender/goal-oriented — focuses on the writer's purpose and product features


Receiver/process-oriented — focuses on the reader's needs and benefits

The prediction (from theory): Dutch readers — from a culture valuing directness — should prefer the concise, sender-oriented style. British readers — from a culture valuing politeness — should prefer the wordy, receiver-oriented style.

Quick Check 2

Which of the following accurately describes the two style dimensions studied by Hendriks et al.?

✓ Correct! The two dimensions were: (1) how concise or wordy the text was, and (2) whether the writing was oriented towards the sender's goals or the receiver's needs. These were chosen because theory predicts they interact with cultural value orientations like individualism-collectivism.
Not quite. The specific dimensions Hendriks et al. used were conciseness (concise vs. wordy) and orientation (sender/goal-oriented vs. receiver/process-oriented). While related concepts like directness exist in the literature, these were not the dimensions directly manipulated in the study.
Section 3 of 3 — Findings & Implications

What Did They Find?

The results were more nuanced than theory predicted. While some cultural differences did emerge, on the whole the two groups evaluated the newsletter styles surprisingly similarly.


Where differences did appear:

Great Britain: A sender/goal-oriented style led to higher purchase intention than in the Netherlands — British readers found it more persuasive.

Netherlands: A concise style was rated more attractive than in Great Britain — Dutch readers preferred shorter, more direct text.

Where no significant differences appeared:

Both groups rated all four style versions as equally comprehensible. A receiver-oriented style generated similar purchase intention in both countries. A wordy style was equally attractive in both countries.


Bottom line: The theoretical benefits of style congruency were not strongly supported. Adapting writing style to cultural preferences had limited effects on how persuasive documents were rated.

Implications for Practice

So what should international organizations actually do?


Hendriks et al. offer a nuanced recommendation. They acknowledge that some localization may be worthwhile (e.g., being slightly more concise for Dutch audiences), but ultimately favour a globalization strategy:

✳️ Use the same styled documents across different markets — because the effects of stylistic adaptation are limited and do not justify the costs of producing multiple localized versions. Where organizations do want to localize, the authors recommend forming multicultural writing teams to develop differently styled documents for different countries.

This is an important finding for professional communicators: spending significant resources on stylistic localization may not deliver the persuasive gains that theory predicts.

Final Quiz

What was Hendriks et al.'s main practical recommendation for international organizations?

✓ Excellent! The key insight is that while cultural differences in style preference exist in theory, the empirical evidence for their impact on persuasion is limited. The cost-benefit analysis therefore favours standardization — with targeted, minor adaptations where clear differences exist (like conciseness for Dutch audiences).
Not quite. While the authors acknowledge that some localization may be useful, they found the effects of stylistic adaptation were limited overall. Their ultimate recommendation leans towards a globalization strategy — using the same documents — because the returns on full localization don't justify its costs.
